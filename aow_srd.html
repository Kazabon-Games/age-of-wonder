Preface
Before You Begin
What this game is. How it works. What makes it different.
Age of Wonder is a tabletop roleplaying game.

A tabletop roleplaying game — TTRPG — is a structured form of collaborative storytelling. One person serves as the Game Master. They describe the world, voice its inhabitants, adjudicate consequences, and advance the story. Everyone else plays a character — a person in that world with a history, a will, and a stake in what happens. Together they build something neither could produce alone.

There are no winners in a TTRPG. There is no board, no elimination, no final score. What there is: a story that belongs to everyone at the table, shaped by decisions that carry real weight because those decisions have real consequences in the world the group shares.

What Diceless Means

Most tabletop roleplaying games use dice to resolve uncertainty. You attempt something, you roll, the number determines the outcome. This produces a certain kind of drama — the tension of randomness, the elation of a natural twenty, the devastation of a critical failure at the worst possible moment.

Age of Wonder does not use dice.

Outcomes are determined by the weight of choices,
the architecture of the character,
and the consequences already in motion.
The Core Principle · Age of Wonder

In Age of Wonder, what your character can do is determined by what they chose to become during the five years before the story begins. How well they do it is determined by the state they are in — their willstrain, their stamina, their leverage, their current position relative to the stakes. The GM adjudicates outcomes by reading the situation honestly against the system. Not randomly. Coherently.

This produces a different kind of drama. Not the tension of luck but the tension of preparation meeting consequence. A character who trained for three years with a blade and developed a signature technique walks into a fight differently than one who spent those years in the library. The difference is not a modifier to a die roll. It is a fundamentally different situation producing fundamentally different options.

The Four Encounter Types

Every session of Age of Wonder takes place within one or more of four structured encounter contexts. Each context has its own resource, its own states, its own stakes.

Combat Presence-based physical confrontation. Resource: Presence — Read, Commit, Hold. Stakes: wound states, stamina degradation, and in the right circumstances, death.
Politics Leverage-based social navigation. Resource: Leverage — a relational web of recognition scores with every significant NPC and faction in Shemsara. Stakes: legitimacy, debt, access, and the arc one threshold that determines whether the heir earns their seat.
Caravan Momentum-based travel and logistics. Resource: Momentum — Route, Cohesion, Cover. Stakes: delayed arrivals, compromised cargo, scattered parties, and capture.
Exploration Depth-based navigation of ruins and unknown spaces. Resource: Depth — the relationship between the space's danger level and the party's current capability. Stakes: loot, spell fragments, technique scrolls, and what the previous civilizations left behind.

Magic is not an encounter type. It is a capability that operates across all four encounter contexts. The Magic chapter covers what magic is and how it functions. Each encounter chapter covers how magic integrates within that specific context.

Reader's Note

How to Use This Document

This document is organized as a reference. You do not need to read it cover to cover before playing. Players should read the Preface, Chapter One, and their own school's entry in Chapter Two before session one. The encounter chapters can be read as each encounter type becomes relevant to your character's story.

The GM should read everything. Not because every piece of information is immediately useful but because the systems are designed to work together, and understanding how they connect produces better adjudication at the table.

Terms that appear in green with an arrow are cross-references — they link to the glossary definition or the chapter where the concept is treated in full. Use them. The system is a coherent whole and the connections between concepts are part of how it works.

I
Chapter One
The Kingdom of Aetheria Nova
The world as the heirs know it walking in. The surface before anyone starts pulling threads.

You arrive in Shemsara at thirteen. You leave at eighteen changed by five years of study, court politics, and the weight of who your family is. You return — if you return — as an heir who has passed the Awakening. What happens between those two moments is the story Age of Wonder tells.

The Awakening is not only a magical event. By tradition and by law, it is the moment an heir is recognized as having reached the age of reason — the point at which Aetheria Nova considers a person capable of bearing the weight of their own decisions. Before the Awakening, an heir's councillor parent or guardian bears responsibility for what their child does in Shemsara. After it, the heir bears that weight themselves. There is no supervised period, no probation, no one whose permission is required. An heir who has passed the Awakening makes their own appointments, spends their own political capital, and answers for their own consequences from the first session forward.

Aetheria Nova is a large kingdom on the continent of Wonderland. It has been at peace for longer than most of its current inhabitants have been alive. The peace is not fragile exactly — there is no obvious threat on the horizon, no neighboring kingdom massing forces, no famine pressing at the gates. It is the kind of peace that becomes invisible through familiarity, which is to say the kind that would be genuinely surprising to lose.

The kingdom is governed from Shemsara, the capital, where King Hector holds court and the Small Council meets. Most of the kingdom's actual administrative complexity lives in the six noble houses — each controlling a city and, by extension, the land and people around it. The houses send their heirs to Shemsara. The heirs study. The crystal speaks. The cycle continues.

Shemsara

Shemsara is the seat of everything that matters in Aetheria Nova. The palace sits at the center. The districts extend outward from it, each one controlled or influenced by the noble house whose character most closely mirrors what that district does. The city is walled. The wall is maintained. Beyond the wall on all sides is the Outskirts — territory that is technically within Shemsara's jurisdiction and practically outside anyone's reliable control.

The city is old enough to have accumulated the kind of institutional weight that makes it hard to change quickly. The Scholars Guild has been keeping records since before the current dynasty. The Church of the Axiom has been adjudicating the moral dimensions of magic since before the Scholars Guild had a building to keep records in. The Merchant Consortium has been controlling harbor access since there was a harbor worth controlling. These institutions do not move fast. They do not need to. They have outlasted faster things.

For an heir arriving at thirteen this is the most complex social environment they have ever encountered. For an heir leaving at eighteen it is the environment they have learned to navigate. The gap between those two states is the measure of what five years in Shemsara produces.

The Small Council of Shem

The Small Council is the advisory body to King Hector. Six noble house representatives hold permanent seats. The council meets formally twice per season and informally whenever the kingdom's business requires it, which is more often than the formal schedule suggests. The council advises on military deployment, trade policy, magical regulation, succession matters, and any dispute between the houses that rises to the level of royal attention.

The council is not a democracy. King Hector is not bound by its advice. He listens carefully and rules as he sees fit. In practice his rulings align with council majority more often than not, which has produced in some councillors the comfortable assumption that the council governs and the king reigns. This assumption has been wrong before and will be wrong again.

Each house heir studies in Shemsara with the explicit understanding that they are being prepared to eventually take their parent's seat. What they are being prepared for is not the formal business of the council. That is knowable from records and study. What they are being prepared for is the informal business — the conversations that happen before the formal meetings, the alliances that form over meals rather than in chambers, the information that moves through the Courier Network at speeds the council record cannot track.

The Small Council governs the kingdom. The heirs govern the Small Council. This is not written anywhere. It does not need to be.

The Districts of Shemsara

The city is divided into seven districts plus the Outskirts. Six districts correspond to the six noble house types — each house controls the district that reflects their home city's character. The seventh is the City Park, a neutral green space that belongs to no house and therefore to everyone. The castle sits at the center, technically its own territory, practically the organizing principle around which everything else arranges itself.

The Military DistrictGarrison, walls, armory. Controls the soldiers of Shemsara and the maintenance of the city's physical defenses. The Watch Commander operates from here. The house controlling this district has the most direct relationship with the City Watch faction.
The Trade DistrictMarkets, harbor, counting houses. Controls the flow of wealth into and out of the capital. The Merchant Consortium's guildmaster maintains offices here. The harbor gives this district a relationship with the Dockworkers Union that the controlling house either cultivates or ignores at their peril.
The Religious DistrictTemple, shrines, clergy. The Church of the Axiom maintains its primary Shemsara presence here. This district's controlling house has a natural relationship with the Church's legitimacy apparatus — useful for heirs seeking endorsement at the legitimacy threshold.
The Scholarly DistrictArchive, academy, scriptorium. The Scholars Guild and the Archivist General operate from here. Historical records, magical theory, the registry of awakened casters, and any document the Guild has decided belongs in the long memory of the kingdom lives in this district. Information that lives here is controlled. Access is granted by the Guild's discretion.
The Agricultural DistrictGranary, trade guilds, water authority. Controls the capital's supply chains. Less politically glamorous than the other districts and therefore often underestimated. A house that controls water and grain in a siege has more leverage than one that controls armies.
The Magical DistrictThe Awakening Sanctum, the caster registry, the foundational magical infrastructure of the city. The crystal that reads every heir at eighteen lives here. The house controlling this district has a relationship with the Church of the Axiom — the two institutions share jurisdiction over what magic means in a moral sense — that produces either productive collaboration or sustained tension.
The OutskirtsTechnically within Shemsara's walls on the map. Practically outside the barrier's reliable coverage and outside any house's direct control. The Outskirts Broker operates here. Information, goods, and people that cannot move through official channels move through the Outskirts. Every heir who spends time here gains access to things unavailable through legitimate means. Every heir who spends time here also becomes visible to people who prefer to operate in the dark.

Beyond the Capital

Aetheria Nova is a large kingdom. Most of it is not Shemsara. Six noble houses govern six cities, each with its own character — military, mercantile, religious, scholarly, agricultural, or magical — that determined which district their heir claimed in the capital. Between and beyond those cities is farmland, forest, road, and the kind of space that a kingdom at peace does not think about very much.

The ruins are out there. Three previous civilizations left physical traces in the land — the Architects, who built machines that still operate; the Predecessor Courts, whose political structures preceded Aetheria Nova's founding; and a third whose name the Scholars Guild does not use in public documents. The ruins they left are known to exist. What they contain is information the Guild controls carefully. Explorers who go looking find things. What they find depends on how deep they go and how prepared they are for what depth produces.

The existence of ruins is common knowledge. Their contents are not. An heir who asks the Archivist General about specific ruins will receive a polite and thorough answer about the general historical record and nothing actionable about what to expect inside one. This is not obfuscation. It is institutional caution born from experience with what happens when people go in unprepared.

The roads between cities are maintained. Travel is not particularly dangerous in peacetime. The Courier Network uses them constantly. Caravans move goods between houses with regularity. The kingdom functions. The infrastructure of a functioning kingdom is easy to take for granted until something threatens it, at which point its value becomes immediately and uncomfortably clear.

Key Figures of Shemsara

These are the people an heir will encounter in the course of navigating Shemsara. Each one has weight in the system. Understanding what they respond to is not manipulation — it is literacy. The Politics chapter covers their mechanical roles in the leverage web in full.

King Hector The Monarch · Seat of Legitimacy Hector has governed Aetheria Nova for over two decades. He is not a figurehead and not a tyrant. He is a capable ruler who has maintained peace through a combination of genuine competence, careful delegation, and a willingness to make decisions that cost him politically when the alternative costs the kingdom more. He responds to demonstrated capability, political stability, and usefulness to the Crown. He does not respond to flattery, inherited reputation, or the assumption that council proximity produces royal favor.
The Royal Chamberlain Keeper of the Royal Calendar · Gate to the King The Chamberlain controls access to King Hector more completely than any other person in Shemsara. A meeting that does not go through the Chamberlain is a meeting that does not happen. They respond to order, discretion, and an heir's demonstrated loyalty to the king above all factional interests. An heir who treats the Chamberlain as an obstacle to be circumvented will find the obstacle remarkably effective. An heir who treats the Chamberlain as a person with their own interests and pressures will find a different kind of relationship available.
The Outskirts Broker Controls the Black Market · Neutral by Survival The Broker has no factional loyalty. They have survived in the Outskirts long enough to understand that survival requires neutrality — selling to all parties equally, owing nothing to any single power, and never being caught holding information that only benefits one side. They respond to coin, favors, and the willingness to operate outside official channels without pretending that is not what is happening. An heir who approaches the Broker with moral hesitation visible in their face will pay more. An heir who approaches without hesitation will be watched more carefully.
The six faction leaders — Guildmaster of the Merchant Consortium, Watch Commander, Dockworkers Foreman, Courier Master, High Priest of the Axiom, and Archivist General — are treated in full in the Politics chapter alongside their faction mechanics. Their brief descriptions here are orientation only.

Chapter Two
Magic
What magic is. How the axiom functions. What it costs a human will to impose change on the world.
Every caster who has tried to argue with the axiom has eventually stopped arguing and started casting. The axiom wins not by debate but by demonstration.

Magic is not a force you channel. It is not energy stored in your body waiting to be released. It is a relationship between your will and a world that responds to it.

The law governing that relationship has one statement:

Production of change in conformity with will.

Every spell you cast is an instance of this law. You define a change. You commit to it completely. The world produces it. That is the whole of magic at its root. Everything else in this chapter is elaboration on what that means in practice and what it costs you.

The axiom was not written. It was discovered — the way gravity was not invented but found. It does not care whether you believe it. It operates regardless.

One further truth the axiom implies and the world confirms: will sufficient to engage the axiom is a human capacity. Other creatures exist in Aetheria Nova. None of them cast. Magic belongs to humans not as a gift but as a consequence of what human will is.

The Nature of Will

Most failed castings are not failures of knowledge. They are failures of commitment. The caster knew what they wanted. They did not fully want it.

Will is not desire. You can desire something without being able to cast it. Will is the complete commitment of your attention and intent toward a defined outcome with no internal resistance remaining.

Will has three components that must be present simultaneously for a casting to succeed.

Clarity

The precise knowledge of what change you are producing. Not approximately. Not generally. Exactly. A vague intent produces vague results or none. Before you cast you must be able to state what will be different about the world when the casting is complete.

Commitment

The absence of doubt, fear, or contradiction about the casting. You are not trying to produce the change. You are producing it. The difference between those two states is the difference between a successful cast and a failed one.

Continuity

Sustaining clarity and commitment for the full duration of the casting. A reflexive spell requires a moment of continuity. A sovereign level casting may require hours. Your ability to sustain will under pressure is the primary measure of your development as a caster.

— ✦ —

When all three are present and aligned the casting succeeds. When any one degrades the casting degrades with it. This is not a rule imposed on magic from outside. It is what magic is.

The Six Schools

There are six schools of magic. Each is a different axis along which will can produce change. A school is not a limitation — it is a direction. A caster aligned to one school can access others at tier three, but the primary school is where the will is deepest and the costs are lowest. Tap any school card to read the full description.

School affinity is determined by the Awakening crystal at age eighteen. The crystal does not assign — it reads five years of choices and reveals the school the will has already become. A caster does not choose their school. They discover it.

The Tier System

A spell's tier is fixed to the operation. Not to the caster. A master and an apprentice casting the same spell are casting the same weight. The master simply carries it better.

Tiers measure the weight of a casting. A spell's tier is determined by four qualities of the operation itself — not the caster, not the context. These qualities are permanent properties of the spell.

QualityWhat It Measures
ScopeWhat the spell acts on and how much of it. Acting on a single will is narrow in size but vast in scope — will is the most resistant substrate magic touches.
PrecisionHow exactly the change must be defined for the spell to function correctly. High precision spells fail if the caster's definition is slightly off.
ResistanceHow hard the world pushes back against this particular change. Some changes are with the grain of reality. Others are against it.
DurationHow long the will must maintain full coherence for the operation to complete and hold. Longer conformity required means higher tier.

Advancement

Advancement is not granted. It is recognized. Something has changed in the caster and the table acknowledges what is already true.

Advancement is not automatic and not granted by time. It is recognized when your will has developed to the point where the next tier's demands are within your reach. Every caster moves through three phases within each tier.

I
Effort
Every casting at this tier is deliberate. The three will components require conscious assembly before each cast. The beat between decision and execution is visible. Willstrain accumulates faster than it should because the caster spends will on assembly as well as the casting itself.
II
Fluency
The assembly overhead is gone. The caster casts at their tier without visible preparation. Willstrain now reflects the honest cost of the spell. This is the longest phase. The will is not just carrying the weight — it is learning the texture of it. Pursue a second school here.
III
Pressure
The tier ceiling castings feel lighter. Not easier in terms of demand — the spell still requires what it requires. But the will meets the demand with something left over. The GM sees this before the player names it. Advancement questions begin here.
— ✦ —

The Advancement Questions

The GM's signal to begin the advancement questions is behavioral, not mechanical. Three things appear together in the Phase of Pressure that do not appear together earlier. The caster's highest-tier spells complete without visible effort — no pause before committing, no recovery after. The caster begins spontaneously attempting variations at the edge of their current tier rather than staying with proven forms — they are probing the ceiling from below without being asked to. And the caster describes what they are doing in terms of what the spell requires of them rather than what it does in the world — they have shifted from output-focused to process-focused understanding. When the GM sees all three the advancement questions begin. Any one of them alone is not sufficient.

When a caster enters the Phase of Pressure the GM begins asking — not announcing. Three questions determine readiness.

What does this casting require of you that a tier one casting does not? A precise answer without hesitation signals developed clarity about their own will.

What would the next tier demand that this tier does not? If they can perceive the shape of what comes next, they are already approaching it.

What in you would need to be different to carry that weight? This is the hardest question and the most important. A genuine answer rather than a theoretical one means advancement is close.

Advancement is confirmed when the caster successfully casts above their ceiling under genuine pressure — not in a controlled setting but in a moment where the situation demands it and their will meets the demand.

Multi-School Access

You begin with one school at tier one. A second school becomes available at tier three in your primary school — begin the second school at tier one. Cross-school casting opens when you reach tier two in a second school. A third school is possible but rare. Most casters develop in one or two schools for their entire career.

Dissolution and Advancement

Partial Dissolution permanently fixes the dissolved caster's tier ceiling at whatever tier was active when threshold was crossed. The ceiling is fixed. This is why Dissolution is not merely a setback. It is a permanent redrawing of what is possible.

Willstrain

Willstrain is not the cost of the spell. It is the cost of being the kind of creature that can cast the spell. Those are different burdens.

Every casting costs something. That cost is called Willstrain — the wear on your capacity for sustained will produced by the act of imposing change on the world. It is not physical exhaustion though it can feel like it. It is the thinning of the self. A caster who does not understand Willstrain will eventually discover it in the worst possible moment.

Stage One
Thinning
Fully functional. Reflexive spells require conscious effort. Margin between intent and outcome has narrowed but is still reliable. Most casters operate here and recover overnight.
Stage Two
Fraying
Will components begin to decohere. Clarity requires effort. Commitment wavers under stress. Continuity shortens. Casting above tier three becomes unreliable. Rest now.
Stage Three
Slippage
Castings produce unconformed change — outcomes adjacent to intent but not identical. The channel between will and world has degraded. You are a danger to yourself and those nearby. Stop casting.
Stage Four
Severance
The world cannot hear your will clearly. Castings fail or produce wildly unconformed results. Physical symptoms appear. This is a medical emergency. Casting at stage four risks Dissolution.

Tracking Willstrain at the Table

Willstrain is tracked narratively through three signals. Watch for all three.

Casting ease. When spells that should be reflexive start requiring deliberate effort — stage one has begun.

Outcome fidelity. When spells start producing results that are close to but not exactly what was intended — stage three has arrived.

Felt coherence. When thinking becomes imprecise, intent becomes hard to hold, and attention fragments under pressure — the caster is advancing through the stages.

Recovery

Willstrain recovers through the cessation of willing against the world. Sleep is the primary mechanism. Deep meditative stillness is the second. Strong emotion, unresolved conflict, and intense desire slow recovery — you are still exerting will even when you are not casting. There are no shortcuts that do not carry cost.

Unconformed Change

When will degrades mid-casting the world does not stop responding. It responds to whatever partial or imprecise signal is being produced. The general principle for adjudication: the world produces a change adjacent to intent in the direction of least resistance. Stage one unconformity produces minor variance. Stage three produces results that may target the wrong object, open to the wrong location, or produce the opposite of the intended behavior.

Dominion school casters face a specific additional risk called blowback — when a Dominion casting produces unconformed change, the target's will can press back through the degraded channel and damage the caster's own will components directly. Blowback Dissolution is the fastest onset Dissolution type known.

Magic in Combat

A caster who understands only willstrain understands magic at rest. A caster who understands magic under physical pressure understands something closer to the truth of what they are.

The spell system covers what magic costs in isolation. Combat adds a second pressure system running simultaneously. A caster in a fight is managing willstrain from casting and stamina from physical confrontation at the same time. Understanding how these interact is not optional knowledge for a caster who intends to survive.

The Action Economy

Every character in a combat exchange has three action slots — Move, Act, and React. Casting uses the Act slot. How many additional slots a spell consumes depends on its tier.

TierSlot CostWhat You Sacrifice
T1Act slot onlyNothing. Move and React remain available.
T2Act + cannot MoveYou hold position to cast. Repositioning is not available this exchange.
T3Act + one other slotYou cast and either Move or React — not both. Choose before declaring.
T4All three slotsYou are fully committed. You cannot Move, Act on anything else, or React to incoming action this exchange.
T5–T6All three slots per exchangeThe casting spans multiple exchanges. You are out of the fight until it completes.

This is why tier four is the most dangerous casting state in a fight. A weapons fighter who understands action economy will identify when a caster has committed all three slots and close distance in that exchange. The caster cannot React. The hit lands. The willstrain from the tier four cast is now compounding with physical pressure.

Wound States and Casting

A Head wound degrades the Read component of presence regardless of stamina state. Read is the accurate perception of what is happening — in casting it means maintaining precise clarity of intent throughout the spell. A caster with a Head wound operating above tier two is producing spells under degraded clarity. The risk of unconformed change increases even when willstrain is low.

A Presence wound drops all three presence components one stage immediately. For a caster this matters because continuity — the sustained coherence required to hold a spell through its duration — degrades with it. A Presence wound mid-cast on a sustained spell risks unconformed change or outright failure depending on severity.

Torso wounds cause stamina to degrade faster. A caster with a Torso wound will reach Spent stamina sooner than expected. At Spent stamina, sustained concentration on tier three or above casting may no longer be viable — the GM adjudicates based on the situation.

Hybrid Casting

A caster can attempt to cast and fight in the same exchange — but only if their session zero training specifically supports it. Players who chose the Wand or Staff weapon specialization and trained in a bridging style — Duelist-Mage, Spell and Steel, Quickcaster, Battlemage, Warden, or Channeler — have earned this capability. Their Act slot can accommodate a tier one or two spell and a weapon action simultaneously. The slot cost is additive. Without this training the caster must choose each exchange: spell or weapon, not both.

Hybrid casting never reduces willstrain costs. The spell costs what it costs. Training changes whether the physical and magical actions can share an exchange — not whether they are cheaper.

The Wand in Combat

The wand specialty grants cast acceleration — all spells are treated one tier lower for slot cost only. Willstrain cost remains at the spell's actual tier. A tier two spell through a wand costs only the Act slot. A tier three spell costs Act plus one other slot. This makes the wand the premier combat implement for casters who want to remain mobile while casting.

A non-caster who chose Wand specialization gains Interruption instead — once per exchange they can spend their React slot to interrupt an opponent's casting action. The spell fails without producing unconformed change. A non-caster wand fighter who understands action economy is one of the most dangerous opponents a caster can face.

The City Barrier

Inside Shemsara and any walled city with magical infrastructure, the barrier prevents death but does not affect magic. A caster can cast any spell they have access to inside the city. The barrier simply ensures that combat ends at incapacitation rather than death. Outside the barrier — in the wilderness or the Outskirts — unconformed change at stage three willstrain has no safety net. The world produces what the degraded will commands.

Magic Against Non-Human Entities

Demons are ex nihil constructs — generated from outside the order of things entirely. The axiom governs the relationship between will and the world. Demons are not of the world in the conventional sense and did not arrive through conjuration. What this means for casting against them is a question the system holds open deliberately. Negation attempting to remove the world's permission for something to continue operates on things the world permitted in the first place. Whether a demon was ever permitted by the world's order is the question every caster who faces one will eventually have to answer through action rather than theory. The Combat chapter covers demon encounters in full.

Dissolution

The fully Dissolved caster still has will. They still want things. They simply cannot impose those wants on the world with enough weight to matter. That is its own kind of grief.

Dissolution is the permanent reduction of your will capacity. Not death. Not madness. Something quieter and in some ways worse — the fixing of a new and lower ceiling on what your will can sustain, impose, and maintain. You can still cast after partial Dissolution. You simply cannot cast at the tiers you once could. The ceiling does not recover. This is why experienced casters treat Willstrain with more respect than any other cost the system levies.

Clarity Dissolution
Commitment and continuity remain intact. Precision of intent is permanently impaired. Castings become approximations. The caster migrates toward schools that tolerate imprecision and away from Divination and targeted Conjuration.
Commitment Dissolution
Clarity and continuity remain. An internal wall forms at the moment of full investment. The caster can intend and can sustain but cannot close the gap between wanting and committing. Some recover partial function through sustained psychological work. Most do not.
Continuity Dissolution
Clarity and commitment remain fully intact. Sustained will capacity collapses. Tier ceiling drops to two or three regardless of prior mastery. The caster knows exactly what they want and wants it completely. They cannot hold it long enough for the world to fully respond.
— ✦ —

Full Dissolution — the complete collapse of magical capacity — is rare. It requires either a single catastrophic overextension at tier six or repeated partial Dissolutions compounding over time. A fully Dissolved caster retains will as an ordinary person retains it. The resonance connection does not disappear. It becomes too narrow to carry any weight of significance.

Avoid Dissolution. It is permanent.

The Moral Flags

The flags are not a cage. They are a mirror. A caster who looks away from that mirror has already answered the question the flag was asking.

The moral flags are not a legal system. They are a map of where magic touches contested territory — will, autonomy, truth, existence. A spell carries a flag not because it is evil but because its execution raises questions the axiom alone cannot answer.

When a player reaches for a flagged spell the table pauses. Not to adjudicate legality — to create space for the question the flag is asking.

Restricted
The spell cannot do what it does without crossing a boundary the target has not consented to and cannot meaningfully resist. Acts on a person's body, perception, identity, or will in ways they did not invite. The caster bears full moral weight for this casting in a way unrestricted spells do not demand.
Have you accounted for what you are taking from this person by casting this spell?
Banned in Most Houses
The spell produces harm that is not incidental to a legitimate purpose but is structurally the purpose itself, or the execution is so violating that no legitimate purpose can justify routine access to it. Banned does not mean impossible. It means the caster has stepped outside what organized magical society has agreed to permit in ordinary practice.
What legitimate purpose does this serve that a restricted spell cannot accomplish? What does it mean that I am willing to do this?
Theoretical Only
No confirmed successful casting exists. The spell is present in the compendium because the axiom permits it in principle and because practitioners have attempted it. It is not a table-available option in ordinary play. Its presence in the canon acknowledges the edges of what magic can theoretically reach.

A Note on Dominion

More Dominion spells carry flags than any other school. This is not a judgment on its practitioners. Every other school acts on matter, space, knowledge, or existence. Dominion acts on will — the very substrate the axiom says magic requires. A school that acts on will operates closer to the root of the system than any other, producing changes that are more intimate, more resistant, and more consequential. A Dominion caster who understands this and develops accordingly is among the most formidable and trustworthy practitioners anywhere. A Dominion caster who does not is among the most dangerous.

Adjudicating Tier — The Borderline Call

A player wants to cast a spell that alters another person's emotional state — calming extreme fear without their awareness. This is Alteration acting on the target's neurological state without consent, affecting their will-adjacent capacity. The GM considers: scope is a single person's internal state (moderate), precision is high because calming fear specifically requires distinguishing it from other emotional states, resistance is high because internal states resist external modification, duration is brief but acts on the target while unwilling. The flags marker applies — this is Restricted territory. The tier lands at three, possibly four depending on how much the target is resisting. The player decides whether to proceed with full knowledge of the weight they are picking up.

Chapter Three
Politics
Legitimacy is the accumulation of recognized authority. How to accumulate it. What it costs not to.
The throne does not care whether your leverage was earned honestly. It cares whether you have enough of it. The council is not a court of virtue. It is a court of weight.

Every political system runs on a theory of why some people get to tell other people what to do. In Aetheria Nova that theory has one statement:

Legitimacy is the accumulation of recognized authority.

Recognition isn't the same as being liked or agreed with. It's the acknowledgment — from the right people, in sufficient quantity — that you have the standing to act in this space. An heir who is feared has recognition. An heir who is owed favors has recognition. An heir who has done something impressive enough that even enemies admit it has recognition. An heir who has done nothing that anyone needs to acknowledge has none.

The politics system is the mechanical expression of this principle. Every choice, every encounter, every relationship at the table either adds to or subtracts from an heir's recognized authority in Shemsara. The arc one endpoint — the legitimacy threshold — is the moment when recognition has accumulated enough that a council seat becomes unavoidable. The throne does not grant that seat willingly. It simply runs out of justifications to withhold it.

Politics is not the same as morality. The legitimacy threshold does not ask how an heir accumulated their recognition — only whether they have accumulated enough. An heir who reaches +3 with King Hector through blackmail and an heir who reaches it through genuine service have met the same requirement. The system records the score. The story records how they got there. Both matter. Only one is mechanical.

Leverage

A leverage score of zero does not mean they have forgotten you. It means you have not yet given them a reason to remember you specifically. That is a more uncomfortable position than being disliked.

Leverage is the primary resource of the politics encounter type. It is a relational web — one score per significant NPC and faction in Shemsara, ranging from -5 to +5. The score represents the degree of recognized authority the heir holds in that relationship at this moment. It is not permanent. It is not personal. It is political.

What the Score Means

A score of zero means no standing — the heir is present in Shemsara but has not yet given this NPC or faction a reason to form a position about them. That sounds neutral. It isn't — it's invisible. An heir at zero with the Watch Commander has no leverage with the city's enforcement apparatus in either direction. They cannot ask for help and they have not yet made an enemy. Invisibility has a short shelf life in a political environment.

Positive scores mean the heir has accumulated some form of recognition — the NPC or faction acknowledges their standing, owes them something, or has reason to support their advancement. A +2 is functional. A +3 is a relationship worth protecting. A +5 means the NPC has invested something in the heir's success and will act on that investment without being asked.

Negative scores mean the heir has made their presence felt in the wrong direction. A -1 is coolness that can be repaired. A -3 is active opposition that will manifest in the heir's political life whether they engage with it or not. A -5 means this entity is an active enemy who will create problems for the heir as a matter of course.

How Leverage Moves

Leverage scores change through four mechanisms. Political actions — deliberate moves the heir makes in conversation or through their contacts — are the primary driver. Conversation outcomes — the four possible results of any significant NPC interaction — move scores in both directions depending on how the encounter resolves. Consequences of other encounter types — a caravan that arrives safely with faction goods, a combat victory that becomes visible, an exploration find that the Scholars Guild hears about — produce leverage movement the heir did not directly seek. And solo session actions move leverage between main sessions without the other heirs present to observe or respond.

Leverage scores do not move silently. When a score changes the GM records it and reflects it in the world — an NPC who was warm becomes slightly cooler, a faction whose contact has stopped returning messages is signaling something. The heir may not know immediately what moved or why. That uncertainty is the information the politics system is designed to produce.

Leverage Ceilings

No score can exceed +5 or fall below -5. These are not mechanical limits — they are political realities. A relationship at +5 has reached the maximum investment any institutional actor will make in a single heir before the costs of that investment begin to outweigh the benefits. A relationship at -5 has reached the point where repair is no longer possible through normal political means. Something structural would have to change — the nature of the conflict, the identity of the people involved, or the circumstances of the world — before the score could move again.

The Six Factions

Factions do not have opinions about heirs. They have interests. The heir who understands the difference will spend their time differently than the one who does not.

Six institutional factions control the operational life of Shemsara. Each controls a domain of city life that every heir eventually needs to engage with. Faction leverage scores move differently from NPC scores — factions respond to demonstrated utility and consistent engagement over time rather than individual conversations. A single impressive action rarely moves a faction score by more than one point. Sustained engagement over multiple sessions can move it substantially.

Merchant Consortium Controls Trade · Harbor · Wealth Flows The oldest continuous institution in Shemsara after the Grand Library. They have survived four dynasties by being indispensable to every one of them. The Consortium does not take political positions publicly. It manages the infrastructure that everyone else's political positions depend on. Responds to commercial advantage, competitor intelligence, harbor stability, and any heir who makes their trade routes function more smoothly than before. Does not respond to ideological appeals.

An heir who has the Church's endorsement has something no amount of leverage buys elsewhere: the implication that their authority is not merely political. The Broker knows who comes. The Broker tells no one — for now. The Outskirts responds to coin and to the willingness to operate without pretending otherwise. The Broker specifically responds to information trades, favors that have future value, and heirs who do not waste their time. The Outskirts does not respond to loyalty — it does not offer any and does not expect it.
Faction leverage scores affect what is available to an heir in political encounters. A faction at +2 or above will provide active assistance when asked — information, endorsement, logistical support within their domain. A faction at 0 provides nothing unless paid. A faction at -2 or below will create friction — slowing processes, withholding cooperation, or providing information to the heir's rivals. The legitimacy threshold requires three factions at +2 or above. Which three is the heir's choice.

Political Actions

The tier of an action reflects its ambition. Ambition without preparation is just exposure.

Political actions are the deliberate moves heirs make to change the leverage web. Each action has a tier that reflects its scope, cost, and consequence. Higher tier actions produce larger effects and carry larger risks. They also require established standing — you cannot execute a tier four maneuver with a leverage score of zero. The web has to support the move.

Every political action concludes with one of the four conversation outcomes. The tier determines what is possible. The outcome determines what actually happens.

Conversation Mechanics

Every conversation has a price. Sometimes the price is invisible until later.

Every significant political interaction — any encounter with a world NPC or faction representative that carries intent — resolves to one of four outcomes. The GM determines the outcome by reading the situation honestly against the NPC's interests and the heir's current standing. The heir's approach, preparation, and current leverage score all factor in. So does what else is happening in the world that session.

Recognition Gained The interaction produced something the NPC or faction values. The relevant leverage score increases by the tier of the action attempted — 1 for T1/T2, 2 for T3, varies for T4. The relationship has moved. The heir has established something to build on.
Neutral The interaction produced nothing in either direction. The heir was present, the NPC was present, something was said, and the score did not move. Neutral is not failure — it is information. It tells the heir that this approach with this NPC at this moment did not land. The score will not move until something changes.
Recognition Lost The interaction produced something the NPC or faction found damaging, insulting, or counterproductive to their interests. The relevant leverage score decreases. How much depends on what was said and how badly it landed. A recognition loss at -1 is recoverable. A recognition loss that drops a score to -3 requires significant repair work before the relationship becomes useful again.
Debt Created The interaction produced recognition but at a cost — the heir has accepted an obligation. The NPC or faction has done something, provided something, or committed to something, and the heir now owes them a return of equivalent value. Debt is tracked by the GM. Unpaid debts accumulate interest in the form of leverage pressure — the creditor becomes less cooperative over time until the debt is settled. A debt ignored long enough becomes a recognition loss.

What the GM Is Reading

The GM adjudicates conversation outcomes by asking three questions about every significant political interaction. What does this NPC or faction actually want right now — not generally but specifically in this moment given what else is happening. Does what the heir brought to this interaction address that want. And does the heir's current leverage score with this NPC or faction give them the standing to have this conversation at all.

An heir with a score of 0 attempting a T3 action is attempting a maneuver they have not earned. The NPC or faction will respond to the standing they see, not the standing the heir assumes. The outcome will reflect that gap.

The GM never tells a player what outcome is coming before it resolves. The player makes their approach. The GM reads the situation. The outcome is what the world produces. Players who want to predict outcomes more accurately should invest in Divination school spells — Read at tier one can tell an heir whether a specific present condition is true before they commit to an approach. That information has value proportional to the action being considered.

The Legitimacy Threshold

The threshold is not a ceremony. There is no moment where a gong sounds and the heir is declared legitimate. There is only a moment where the argument against giving them the seat becomes harder to make than the argument for it.

The arc one win condition. Four requirements. Any method. The Legitimacy Threshold appendix contains the complete reference. This section explains what each requirement means in play.

King Hector at +2 or above This is the hardest requirement and the one that cannot be rushed. Hector reads people accurately and quickly. An approach that lands with his councillors often does not land with him because he has seen what his councillors respond to and has watched it fail to predict the person behind the approach. The most reliable path is visible action in the kingdom's interest that Hector did not commission. He notices what he does not have to ask for.
Three factions at +2 or above The heir must demonstrate that three of the six factions consider them a useful and trustworthy presence in Shemsara. Which three is entirely the heir's strategic choice — and a revealing one. An heir who cultivates the Church, the Watch, and the Consortium is building a different kind of political career than one who cultivates the Dockworkers, the Couriers, and the Scholars. Both paths lead to the threshold. They arrive at very different seats.
Two of five other councillors at +1 or above The five other house heirs on the council. The heir needs at least two of them to acknowledge their legitimacy as peers — not as allies, not as friends, but as people whose standing they recognize. This requirement creates pressure toward inter-heir engagement and away from purely isolationist strategies. An heir who cultivates only world NPCs and ignores the other players will find this requirement the hardest.
One high-weight endorsement at +3 or above A single relationship of genuine depth with one powerful figure — King Hector, the High Priest of the Axiom, or the Archivist General. A +3 score represents recognition that goes beyond transactional. The NPC has invested something in the heir's success and will act on that investment without being asked. This requirement most rewards consistent relationship-building across many sessions over one-time leverage grabs. It is also the requirement that most reflects what kind of political career the heir is building.
The legitimacy threshold is the arc one win condition. Achieving it does not end the campaign — it opens the next chapter. What comes after an heir earns their seat is a different kind of problem than earning it. The council seat is the beginning of the story the system was always pointing toward.

Checking the Threshold

The GM checks the legitimacy threshold at the end of every main session. If any heir has met all four requirements their arc one story is resolved. The resolution happens in the fiction — not as an announcement but as a political reality that surfaces in play. The other councillors stop finding reasons to delay. The royal calendar makes room. The seat becomes available.

If multiple heirs meet the threshold simultaneously the GM adjudicates which seat becomes available first based on which heir's combined standing is highest weighted by Hector's leverage score. Hector's preference is decisive. That is what it means to be the king.

Magic in Politics

The heirs who use magic in political encounters are the most effective and the most exposed. The schools do not care about this tradeoff. The Sanctum does.

Magic interacts with the politics encounter type through four specific school applications. These are not the only ways magic can be brought to bear in a political context — a caster's imagination is broader than any list. But these four represent the most common and most consequential interactions between Chapter Two's capabilities and the politics system.

◎
Divination — The Information Advantage Divination school spells provide the most direct advantage in political encounters. Read at tier one can determine whether a specific present condition is true — is this NPC lying, is this offer genuine, does this faction leader know what I think they know. Warmth at tier one detects the presence of living creatures without identifying them — useful for determining whether a private conversation is actually private. At higher tiers Divination can trace resonance signatures, read probability fields, and access historical truth that the Scholars Guild has not formally recorded. A Divination caster in a political encounter knows things their counterparts do not. That knowledge is leverage of a different kind than the score system tracks — it is information advantage that shapes every decision.
◈

Solo Sessions

The other heirs are not present. Neither are their excuses. What happens in a solo session happens to a world that cannot immediately respond.

Solo sessions are weekday sessions between main sessions where one heir acts alone. They exist because political maneuvering does not pause between council meetings. Heirs cultivate relationships, gather information, and make moves in the gaps. The main session is where those moves surface as consequences the other players feel without necessarily understanding their origin.

Solo sessions are also where betrayal happens. They are the closest the current campaign has to PvP — not through combat but through political action taken against another heir's position in their absence. The imposter dynamic is the mechanism. Someone moved something. At the main session the effects appear. Nobody immediately knows who acted or how.

✦ Available in Solo Sessions — T1 and T2
Any T1 or T2 action is available in a solo session. The heir introduces themselves to new contacts, cultivates existing relationships, delivers on debts, gathers information through legitimate channels. These are the foundation actions that build the leverage web over time. Solo T1 and T2 actions cannot affect another heir's scores directly — they build the acting heir's position.
✦ Available in Solo Sessions — T3 (Select)
Three categories of T3 action are available in solo sessions. Information actions — using contacts or Divination to gather intelligence about another heir's leverage web, their NPC relationships, or their planned moves. Positioning actions — establishing a presence with an NPC or faction before another heir can, or reinforcing existing standing ahead of a main session confrontation. Outskirts actions — working with the Broker to access black market information, plant misinformation through unofficial channels, or move resources in ways that do not appear in official records.
T3 Outskirts actions in solo sessions are the primary vector for political betrayal. An heir who plants misinformation through the Broker affects another heir's relationship with the NPC who receives that information. The target heir does not know this happened. They discover it when an NPC who was warm becomes cool without explanation. The GM tracks all Outskirts actions and their downstream effects.
✗ Not Available in Solo Sessions — T3 Confrontational · T4 · T5
Coordinated maneuvers, direct confrontations, and removal actions require the main session because they involve consequences that affect other players in real time. An heir cannot force a rival's score into negative territory while that rival is absent and unable to respond. The political system is adversarial. It is not one-sided.

What the GM Tracks Between Sessions

After every solo session the GM updates the leverage web, records any information the acting heir gathered, and determines any downstream effects from Outskirts actions. These effects are prepared but not revealed until the main session produces the moment where they surface naturally. The GM's notes between sessions are the difference between a world that feels coherent and a world that feels managed.

The GM also tracks which heirs have been in the Outskirts and what they traded there. This information lives in the GM's notes. It becomes available to the Broker at any time. If an heir's Outskirts activities become advantageous for the Broker to reveal — to another heir, to a faction, to King Hector — the Broker will consider the offer. Everything in the Outskirts has a price.

Personal NPCs in the Political Web

The session zero NPCs are not background. They are leverage in human form — with all the complications that implies.

Every heir arrives in Shemsara with five personal NPCs defined in their session zero record — the Councillor, the Ally, the Rival, the Contact, and the Wild Card. These personal NPCs interact with the political web in specific ways that differ from world NPC interactions.

The Councillor is the heir's primary political sponsor. Their leverage scores with world NPCs and factions provide a floor that the heir inherits partially — not through starting scores, but through the doors that remain open because the heir's family holds them. When the heir acts in ways that embarrass the Councillor the doors close. When the heir succeeds visibly the Councillor's own scores improve.

The Ally provides access — to a specific faction, to a category of information, or to a resource the heir cannot directly control. Using the Ally costs something. The Ally wants something and an heir who takes without returning will find the relationship cooling toward the Neutral outcome faster than expected.

The Rival is the most direct pressure source in the heir's personal NPC web. The Rival wants something from the heir specifically — their public humiliation, control of a specific NPC relationship, proof of the house shadow, failure at the legitimacy threshold. The GM plays the Rival actively. They make moves in solo sessions. They are the agent most likely to initiate T5 actions against the heir when their leverage reaches that level.

The Contact is the heir's primary access point to the faction most aligned with their dominant aspect choice from session zero. The Contact operates within that faction and can facilitate T2 and T3 actions with that faction that would otherwise require more sessions to establish. The Contact expects consistent return. An heir who activates the Contact repeatedly without delivering on expectations will find the Contact increasingly unavailable.

The Wild Card is the heir's uncertainty variable. Their category, significance, and current status were defined in session zero but their role in play is determined by the GM reading the heir's story as it develops. The Wild Card will surface at the moment the GM determines the heir most needs to be confronted by what they are afraid of or what they are reaching for. This is not adversarial design. It is narrative design. The Wild Card is the system's mechanism for ensuring the heir's session zero choices remain alive in the story rather than becoming backstory.

Chapter Four
Combat
Presence, stamina, wound states. The action economy. What it means to fight when there are no dice.
A fight is not a roll of dice. It is a sequence of decisions made under pressure by people who have trained to make them faster than the other person. Training is what you brought to session zero. Pressure is what the encounter produces.

Combat in Age of Wonder is not resolved by randomness. It is resolved by reading — reading your opponent, reading the situation, and reading your own current state honestly enough to know what you can commit to and what you cannot.

The combat encounter type has one primary resource — Presence — and two state systems that degrade it: Stamina and Wound States. Presence degrades as stamina degrades and as wounds accumulate. A character at full Presence can do everything their training allows. A character at depleted Presence is making decisions with a diminished capacity to execute them. The fight ends when Presence degrades beyond what the situation will sustain — or when the situation changes enough that fighting stops making sense.

A fight in Shemsara is a negotiation conducted with bodies. The outcome reflects who prepared better, who read the situation more accurately, and who had more left when it mattered.

Presence

Read without Commit produces hesitation. Commit without Hold produces overextension. Hold without Read produces stubbornness. All three failures are visible to an opponent who has trained to see them.

Presence is the primary resource of the combat encounter type. It is composed of three components — Read, Commit, and Hold — that must function together for a character to fight effectively. Each component has a specific role in the combat exchange. Each degrades independently under specific conditions. A character can lose Read without losing Commit. They can maintain Hold when everything else is compromised. The pattern of degradation tells the GM and the player what the character can and cannot do as the fight develops.

Read Perceptive Component The accurate perception of what is happening — what the opponent is about to do, what the terrain favors, what the next exchange is likely to produce. Read is what combat training develops first and what stress degrades first. A character with strong Read sees openings. A character with degraded Read misses them. Degrades from: Head wounds · Stamina at Strained or Spent · Surprise or disorientation · Simultaneous overwhelming inputs
Commit Decisiveness Component The decisiveness and force of action — committing to a strike, a technique, or a movement without hesitation that telegraphs intent. Commit is what separates a trained fighter's action from an untrained person's action at the moment of execution. Without Commit the body does what it knows but without the conviction that gives it speed and direction. Degrades from: Weapon Arm wounds · Stamina at Spent · Sustained pressure without recovery · Disarming or disabling actions
Hold Sustained Capacity Component The sustained capacity to maintain defensive response — the ability to react to incoming action, hold ground, and maintain positioning across exchanges. Hold is what makes the difference between a fighter who is hard to land a hit on and one who absorbs damage while trying to attack. It is the last component to degrade and the one whose loss is most immediately dangerous. Degrades from: Shield Arm wounds · Leg wounds affecting positioning · Stamina at Winded and beyond · Presence wounds

Presence in Non-Combat Characters

A character who chose zero Combat aspect years in session zero has not trained Presence under physical pressure. Their Commit and Hold exist as baseline human capacities — they can decide to act and they can maintain a position. What they lack is trained Read. They cannot accurately time actions against an opponent, cannot read openings, and cannot execute techniques that require Read to time correctly.

Inside the city barrier, an heir with zero Combat training who finds themselves in a physical confrontation can spend their Act slot on political reframing — turning the encounter into a conversation the politics system can resolve. This is not a special ability. It is a situation-reading option available to any heir who has the standing and the presence of mind to shift the context before the fight commits fully. Outside the barrier this option is not available. The world outside the walls does not pause for diplomacy.

Stamina

Spent is not the end of the fight. It is the moment the fight becomes purely about survival rather than winning. Some characters are better at that moment than others. Most are not.

Stamina tracks the physical capacity to sustain combat effectively. It degrades through the exchange of blows, sustained exertion, and the cumulative cost of maintaining Presence under pressure. Unlike wound states, stamina degradation is reversible within an encounter given sufficient breathing room — a character who creates distance and holds position for an exchange may recover one stage. Between encounters, stamina resets to Fresh.

Fresh

Winded

Techniques are still available but Hold begins to thin. Sustained pressure costs more. Offensive techniques that depend on timing show caution — they are available but the window for execution is narrower. Recovery within the encounter is possible with one full exchange of distance and non-engagement.

Stamina and Wound Interaction

Torso wounds cause stamina to degrade faster — the character reaches Winded, Strained, and Spent earlier than they otherwise would. A character with a Torso wound who was managing the fight at Fresh may find themselves at Winded after fewer exchanges than expected. The GM tracks this progression and signals it through description before making it mechanical. A character who has trained Read will notice the change in their own capacity before it becomes critical.

Wound States

Six wound states. Six ways the body announces that what was done to it has changed what it can do. The body does not lie about this. The character might.

Six wound states represent specific physical damage accumulated in combat. Each wound state has a precise mechanical consequence — not a general penalty but a specific limitation on what that part of the body can do. Wounds accumulate across exchanges. They do not reset within an encounter. They reset at the start of the next session — field treatment through ALT magic or a medical facility can clear wounds between encounters in the same session.

Weapon Arm Basic weapon actions cost one additional slot. The character can still strike but execution is compromised — the arm cannot deliver with the speed and precision it had before the wound. All offensive techniques that require the weapon arm are unavailable until the wound is treated.
Shield Arm Two-handed defensive applications — blocking with both arms, two-handed weapon grips — are unavailable. One-handed actions remain functional. Techniques requiring both arms unavailable. Staff Barrier Generation is unavailable while Shield Arm is wounded.
Legs Move slot costs one additional slot. The character can still move but repositioning takes longer and is less decisive. Ground cannot be held as effectively. Techniques requiring movement — footwork-dependent styles, approaches that require closing or creating distance — show caution or are unavailable.
Torso Stamina degrades faster throughout the encounter. The character reaches each stamina stage earlier than they otherwise would. No direct technique restriction but the accelerated stamina degradation indirectly restricts technique availability by pushing the character toward Strained and Spent faster.

All techniques that require accurate Read to time — which includes most offensive techniques — show caution. Casting above tier two shows caution because Read underlies casting Clarity under pressure.

This is the most severe single wound state — it represents a blow that has not just damaged the body but has shaken the character's capacity to function in the fight entirely. All techniques become unavailable until presence components recover at least one stage. This requires a full exchange of non-engagement minimum.

Wound Treatment

Within a session wounds persist between encounters unless treated. Two treatment options exist. A medical facility between encounters clears all wounds — the character is physically treated and recovers what they lost. Alternatively, ALT magic at tier two or three can serve as field treatment — Alteration school spells acting on biological tissue can restore structural damage and reduce wound state consequences. The willstrain cost of field treatment is real and must be weighed against the alternative.

At the start of each new session all wound states clear regardless of treatment. The session represents time sufficient for natural recovery between significant encounters. This is a deliberate design choice — wound states create pressure within and between encounters in a session but do not permanently diminish characters across sessions.

The Action Economy

Every exchange has three slots. Three slots is always all there is. What a character can do with three slots reflects everything their session zero choices built.

Every character in a combat exchange has three action slots — Move, Act, and React. These slots represent the physical capacity available in a single exchange of action. Every action a character takes in an exchange costs one or more slots. When slots are used they are gone until the next exchange. The question every exchange answers is: given these three slots and my current presence and stamina state, what is the best use of what I have.

Basic Actions and Techniques

A basic weapon strike costs the Act slot only. It represents a trained attack executed without the additional commitment of a technique — functional, reliable, available even when presence components are degraded. A character at Spent stamina with a Weapon Arm wound can still attempt a basic strike. It will not be impressive. It will cost more. It may not succeed. But the option exists.

Techniques cost Act plus additional slots as defined by the technique. A technique that costs Act and Move requires the character to give up repositioning that exchange. A technique that costs all three slots commits the character completely — they cannot React to incoming action and cannot reposition. The higher cost reflects the higher consequence and the higher potential. Techniques are the difference between a fighter who is difficult to deal with and one who is dangerous.

Only trained characters can execute techniques. Zero Combat aspect years means no techniques — the character's Act slot can produce a basic strike or, inside the barrier, a political reframe. That is all it can produce in a physical confrontation.

Declaring Actions

At the start of each exchange characters declare their intended actions. Declaration is simultaneous — no character gets to see what another has declared before committing their own declaration. This is where Read matters. A character with strong Read is declaring based on accurate perception of what the opponent is likely to do. A character with degraded Read is declaring based on incomplete information and hoping the gap does not cost them.

After declaration the GM resolves the exchange. Actions that interact — a strike into a reaction, a technique into a simultaneous strike — are adjudicated based on the slot costs involved, the presence states of both characters, and the physical logic of what each character has committed to. The result is what the world produces when two sets of choices collide.

Initiative

Initiative is not speed. It is perceptive readiness — the narrow advantage that comes from seeing what is happening fractionally before the other person and having trained the body to respond to that perception without hesitation.

Initiative determines which character's action resolves first when two characters declare conflicting actions in the same exchange. In most exchanges the resolution order is obvious from the physical logic of the situation — a character closing distance does not arrive before they have moved. But in exchanges where both characters declare Act slot actions simultaneously, initiative determines whose action lands first and whether the second action can still resolve or is interrupted by the first.

The Read Declaration

Initiative is determined by the Read component of Presence. At the start of each exchange each character makes a Read declaration — an assessment of what the situation calls for, what the opponent is likely to do, and what the best available response is. The character whose Read declaration is most accurate to the actual situation has initiative that exchange.

The GM adjudicates Read accuracy. A character with strong Read and no Head wound who is reading an opponent they have engaged multiple times in this encounter has significant advantage. A character at Strained stamina with a Head wound reading a new opponent for the first time has almost none.

Dagger Initiative Advantage

The Dagger weapon specialty grants a permanent initiative advantage — in simultaneous Act declarations the Dagger wielder's action always resolves first regardless of the Read comparison. This represents the weapon's characteristics — short, fast, concealable, optimized for the moment before an opponent has fully committed. A Dagger fighter who has trained this specialty has built their entire combat approach around being first in the moment that matters.

Dagger Initiative Advantage is overridden only by Projectile Engagement Denial — a projectile user who has already declared and committed to their action before the Dagger fighter closes distance resolves first because they acted before the dagger was in range.

Multiple Combatants

In encounters with more than two combatants, the GM manages initiative as a sequence of exchanges rather than a strict turn order. Each exchange involves all participants declaring simultaneously. The GM resolves the exchange by identifying which actions interact with which other actions and adjudicating each interaction in the order that physical logic demands. An action that removes a participant from the exchange — knocking them down, creating distance, incapacitating a limb — changes what is available to everyone else in that same exchange.

In multi-combatant situations Spear Reach Dominance and Projectile Engagement Denial become significantly more powerful. A Spear user who forces all opponents to spend Move+React to close is managing multiple threat vectors simultaneously. A Projectile user who can target any participant regardless of position becomes the priority threat in the exchange — which is itself tactically meaningful information for everyone who has to declare simultaneously.

Weapon Specialties

A weapon specialty is not proficiency with a weapon. It is a philosophy of how physical confrontation should be conducted, expressed through a specific implement.

Six weapon specialties are available through Combat aspect choices in session zero. Each specialty grants one permanent mechanical property that applies in every combat encounter where the weapon is present and the character has the slots to engage their specialty. Specialties are not activated — they are always active, always part of how the character occupies a fight.

Sword
Range of Response Once per exchange the Sword fighter can split their Act slot — using it for both an offensive action and a defensive response within the same exchange without needing to spend the React slot for the defensive element. This represents the sword's balance between attack and defense, the discipline of a fighter who does not commit completely to offense because they have been trained to maintain response capacity even while striking. Tactical implication: The Sword fighter is the hardest to catch in a fully committed exchange. They always have something left. This makes them dangerous against opponents who rely on heavy commitment techniques that assume the target cannot respond.
Spear
Reach Dominance An opponent who wants to close to striking range against a Spear fighter must spend both their Move and React slots to do so — Move to close the distance, React to deal with the spear's threat as they enter the weapon's effective range. This leaves the opponent with only their Act slot in the exchange where they close distance. Tactical implication: The Spear fighter controls the exchange before it begins by controlling who can engage and at what cost. In multi-combatant situations this becomes exponentially more powerful — multiple opponents all paying the closing cost simultaneously strips them of response capacity.
Wand
Cast Acceleration (Casters) · Interruption (Non-Casters) For casters: all spells cast through the wand are treated one tier lower for slot cost only. Willstrain cost remains at the spell's actual tier. A tier two spell costs Act only rather than Act plus no Move. A tier three spell costs Act plus one other slot rather than two. The wand provides no physical defense — a caster engaged at close range with a wand has no React option that uses the implement.

For non-casters: once per exchange the wand fighter can spend their React slot to interrupt an opponent's casting action. The spell fails without producing unconformed change. A non-caster wand fighter who understands the action economy is one of the most dangerous opponents a caster can face. Tactical implication: Caster wand fighters are the most mobile casters in a fight — they can cast and still reposition or react. Non-caster wand fighters are anti-caster specialists. Both roles require understanding the casting economy to exploit effectively.
Staff
Barrier Generation Once per encounter the Staff fighter can spend their React slot to absorb one wound state completely — the wound that would have been applied does not apply. This represents the staff's capacity as a defensive implement, the two-handed leverage that allows full interception of an incoming strike or technique. Once used the barrier is expended for that encounter. Tactical implication: The Staff fighter holds a single guaranteed save — one wound that will not land regardless of what else is happening in the exchange. The question is always when to use it. A Staff fighter who uses their barrier early is gambling that nothing more severe comes later. One who holds it too long may never find the right moment.
Dagger
Initiative Advantage In simultaneous Act declarations the Dagger fighter's action always resolves first regardless of the Read comparison. This is permanent and unconditional — degraded Read does not reduce it, Head wounds do not suppress it, Strained stamina does not remove it. The Dagger fighter is always first in the moment of simultaneous commitment. The only exception is Projectile Engagement Denial used before the Dagger is in range. Tactical implication: The Dagger fighter builds their entire approach around this advantage. Techniques are designed for the moment after they have resolved first — Assassin styles in particular depend on the damage and disruption that landing first produces before the opponent can fully commit.
Projectile
Engagement Denial The Projectile fighter can target any participant in the encounter regardless of their physical position relative to that participant. Distance and terrain that would prevent melee engagement do not affect projectile targeting. Reloading costs the Move slot — the Projectile fighter must choose each exchange between maintaining fire capacity and repositioning. Tactical implication: The Projectile fighter does not participate in the melee — they manage it from outside it. Their threat forces every melee participant to consider them when declaring actions. In multi-combatant situations the Projectile fighter becomes the highest-priority threat for anyone who can reach them, which creates its own tactical dynamic.
Hybrid casting — using magic and weapons in the same exchange — requires specific session zero training combining Combat and Magic aspects with a bridging fighting style. The training required and the slot costs involved are covered in Chapter Two: Magic in Combat. Without that training the choice in each exchange is weapon or spell, not both.

Location and Stakes

Inside the barrier you are protected from death. Outside it you are protected from nothing. The Outskirts is inside the wall and outside the barrier. The people who operate there understand this distinction better than most.

Where a combat encounter takes place determines what the worst possible outcome is. Age of Wonder is designed so that the environment regulates the severity of consequences, not just the participants. This means the same fight can be a serious political incident inside the city and a survival emergency outside it.

Inside the City Barrier Combat ends when one participant reaches Spent stamina or has accumulated three wound states — whichever comes first. Death is not possible inside the barrier. The barrier prevents mortal injury from combat. Incapacitation, humiliation, political consequence, and wound states that persist through the session are all possible. Death is not. This is the environment the heirs spend most of arc one operating in.
The Outskirts The Outskirts exists inside Shemsara's walls but outside the barrier's reliable coverage. Blind spots exist throughout — the barrier is not continuous in this territory. Combat in the Outskirts may or may not be covered by the barrier depending on where exactly the encounter occurs. The GM determines barrier coverage per encounter based on where in the Outskirts it takes place. Death is possible. The black market exists here for a reason.
Outside the City No barrier. No protection from mortal consequence. Combat outside the city — on the road, in ruins, in wilderness — operates under full stakes. A character who reaches Spent stamina with significant wounds and no allies in position has a genuine chance of dying. This is the environment where the exploration and caravan encounter types primarily operate and why preparation for those encounters is taken seriously.
Combat Resolution Inside the barrier, combat resolves when a participant reaches the threshold. Outside, combat can continue past Spent — the question becomes whether the losing participant can survive long enough for the situation to change. Flight is always an option. Its success depends on Leg wound status, stamina state, terrain, and what the opponent is willing to commit to pursuit. A Spent character who successfully creates distance and is not pursued has survived. What they do next is the story.

Techniques

A technique is not a special move. It is the expression of a fighter's specific understanding of how to win, developed over years, and available only when the situation produces the conditions that understanding was designed for.

Techniques are trained combat capabilities beyond the basic strike. Every character who chose Combat aspects in session zero has access to techniques derived from their weapon specialization, fighting style, and signature technique choices. Techniques have three defining properties: a trigger condition, a slot cost, and an effect.

The trigger condition must be met before the technique can be executed. A technique that requires the opponent to have committed their Act slot cannot be used in an exchange where they have not committed it — the fighter must wait for the moment the technique was designed for. This is the meaning of trained Read: identifying when that moment has arrived before the opportunity closes.

The slot cost reflects what the technique demands from the character executing it. Higher cost techniques are more powerful and more committing. A technique that costs all three slots is a total commitment — the character cannot react to incoming action and cannot reposition in that exchange. Whether the technique lands determines whether the cost was correct.

Technique Availability by Stamina State

All techniques are available at Fresh stamina. At Winded, Hold-dependent techniques show caution — they are available but the window of execution is narrower and the cost of mistiming is higher. At Strained, Read-dependent techniques become unreliable — the character may commit to the technique and find the timing off. At Spent, no techniques are available. The character is surviving. The body is doing what it can without the trained capacity to execute what the mind knows.

Signature Techniques

The signature technique is the deepest expression of a fighter's approach — developed from three years of Combat aspect study in session zero. Signature techniques represent something the character has spent years developing specifically: a particular timing, a particular exploitation of their weapon's properties, a particular reading of how opponents respond to specific situations.

Signature techniques are recorded on the play sheet with their trigger condition, slot cost, and effect written out in full. During a fight the player should not have to remember these details — the sheet surfaces them. The GM confirms when the trigger condition is met. The player declares the execution. The world produces the result.

A Worked Exchange

Two heirs inside the city barrier. Mira — Sword specialty, two Combat years, Fresh stamina, one technique: Riposte (trigger: opponent commits Act and React in the same exchange; cost: Act; effect: immediate counter-strike that resolves before the opponent can reposition). Davan — Dagger specialty, one Combat year, Fresh stamina, no developed techniques yet.

Declaration. Both players declare simultaneously. Mira declares a basic strike — Act only. Davan declares a basic strike — Act only. Both are committing their Act slot to offense.

Initiative. Simultaneous Act declarations. Davan's Dagger Initiative Advantage means his action resolves first regardless of Read. Davan's strike lands. Mira takes the hit — her GM rules it a Weapon Arm graze, not a full wound, but a warning. Mira's strike still resolves — her Act was committed before Davan's landed.

Next exchange. Mira's player reads the situation. Davan committed both Act and React last exchange — wait, no, he only committed Act. He has Move and React available. But Mira knows Davan's approach: close distance fast, commit everything to the Act. She declares her Riposte technique — costs Act, trigger requires Davan to commit both Act and React. She is betting he will.

Davan's player, feeling confident from the first exchange, declares a full press — Act to strike, React to intercept any counter. He has committed both Act and React.

Resolution. Davan's Dagger Initiative fires first — his strike lands. But the trigger condition for Riposte is now met: Davan committed both Act and React in the same exchange. Mira's Riposte resolves immediately — her counter-strike fires before Davan can reposition. Both characters take hits in the same exchange. Davan, with one Combat year and no techniques to draw on, is now at Winded from the accumulated pressure. Mira has taken two hits in two exchanges. Her Weapon Arm shows caution — offensive techniques will cost one additional slot until treated.

What this exchange illustrates. The Dagger's Initiative Advantage is consistent but does not prevent counter-strikes — it controls resolution order, not outcome. Techniques require waiting for a specific trigger condition and are vulnerable to an opponent who reads what you are waiting for. Stamina and wound state consequences accumulate across exchanges and change what is available before the fight is decided.

This exchange takes place inside the city barrier. Neither character is at risk of death — the fight will end when one reaches Spent stamina or three wound states. Outside the barrier the same exchange carries different stakes. The technique timing and slot logic are identical. What changes is what Spent means when the barrier is not there to catch the fall.

Characters with hybrid casting capability — combat and magic training with a bridging style from session zero — can execute techniques that involve both physical and magical action. These hybrid techniques follow the slot economy of both systems simultaneously. The physical component costs what physical actions cost. The magical component costs what tier casting costs. The total slot demand is additive. See Chapter Two: Magic in Combat for the complete hybrid casting rules.

Non-Human Entities in Combat

A demon does not have presence in the way a human fighter has presence. It does not Read or Commit or Hold in ways that training prepared you for. The action economy applies. Everything else requires on-the-spot adjudication.

The combat system is designed for human versus human encounters. Presence, stamina, wound states, and techniques all emerge from a framework built around how trained human bodies engage each other under pressure. When the opponent is not human the framework partially applies and partially does not.

Animals and non-human creatures of the world operate on presence and stamina — they can be read, they commit to actions, they hold positions. The wound state system applies to them with adjustments for anatomy. The GM adjudicates what a wound to a specific location means for the specific creature. The action economy applies. Initiative applies. Techniques that depend on reading a human opponent's trained patterns may not apply cleanly — a creature that does not use fighting styles cannot be read the same way.

Demons are a different category entirely. Ex nihil constructs do not have presence in the human sense. They do not Read in ways that Read training prepared a fighter to perceive. They do not tire in ways stamina tracks. The action economy applies to the encounter because action economy is the structure of exchange itself, not a property of the participants. But the wound state system, the stamina system, and the technique system all require significant GM adjudication. What constitutes a Head wound on an entity without a head in the conventional sense is a question the system holds open because the answer depends on the specific demon and what it is made of.

Casters facing demons face the additional complexity covered in Chapter Two: Magic in Combat — the question of whether the axiom's jurisdiction extends to ex nihil constructs and what Negation means when applied to something that was not permitted by the world's order in the first place. These questions are narrative design, not mechanical gaps. They are the questions the encounter with a demon is meant to produce.

Demons are not common knowledge in arc one. The heirs arrive at the Awakening Ceremony knowing about magic, politics, and the general shape of the world. Demons are not part of that general shape — they belong to a history the Scholars Guild has classified and the Church of the Axiom discusses in theological rather than tactical terms. An heir who encounters a demon in arc one is encountering something their training did not specifically prepare them for. That is by design.

Chapter Five
Caravan
Momentum across distance. How things move, what threatens them, and what it means to arrive.
In arc one most caravans are small. A courier. A sealed package. A delegation of three. The system does not care about scale. Momentum is momentum whether it is a trade convoy crossing the kingdom or a message moving from the Trade District to the Outskirts Broker without being seen.

The caravan encounter type covers the movement of people, information, and resources from one place to another through a world that has reasons to interfere with that movement. It does not require large groups or long distances. What it requires is that something needs to get somewhere and something else is working against that happening.

A caravan is any movement that has a destination, a threat, and something worth protecting.

In arc one the caravan system primarily governs information movement inside Shemsara — courier runs, message interceptions, quiet package transfers through district networks and the Outskirts. These are small caravans with high political stakes. A message that does not arrive changes the leverage web. A message that arrives but was read on the way changes it differently. A message that arrives but contains misinformation planted by another heir changes it in ways the recipient cannot immediately detect.

The same system that governs a courier run from the Scholarly District to King Hector's Chamberlain will govern, in later arcs, a trade convoy crossing the kingdom under threat of road ambush. The mechanics scale because the underlying structure — momentum built from route, cohesion, and cover — applies at any distance and any group size.

Momentum

Momentum at zero does not mean the caravan has stopped. It means everything required to keep it moving has been stripped away. What happens next is whatever the situation imposes.

Momentum is the primary resource of the caravan encounter type. It measures the caravan's overall capacity to continue moving effectively toward its destination. Momentum is measured 0 to 10. It is composed of three components — Route, Cohesion, and Cover — each contributing to the total.

Route Path Clarity · Alternative Access The clarity and viability of the path from origin to destination. A clear route with established contacts at checkpoints and no hostile territory en route contributes fully to Momentum. A compromised route — one that passes through territory controlled by an unfriendly faction, requires negotiation at each checkpoint, or has no established path at all — reduces Route contribution and slows Momentum gain. At city scale: Route is determined by which districts the movement passes through, who controls those districts, and whether the moving party has the leverage to transit without interference. A message that must pass through a rival heir's district has a compromised Route unless the Courier Network — whose neutrality makes them the only route guaranteed through any district — carries it.
Cohesion Group Integrity · Internal Trust The internal integrity of the moving group — whether the people, documents, or resources in the caravan are moving with consistent purpose and without internal fracture. A courier who has been approached by another heir, a delegation with divided loyalties, a package that has changed hands more times than planned — all reduce Cohesion. High Cohesion means the caravan moves as a single will toward a single destination. At city scale: Cohesion is threatened by the politics system directly. An heir who has cultivated a relationship with a courier or delivery contact creates Cohesion vulnerability in another heir's caravan movements. A Domination school caster who has compromised a messenger's will has eliminated Cohesion entirely for that movement.
Cover Visibility · Protection from Interference The degree to which the movement is shielded from observation and interference. High Cover means the caravan moves without being seen by people who would want to stop it, read its contents, or redirect it. Low Cover means it is visible — the right people know it is moving, roughly where it is, and have opportunity to interfere before it arrives. At city scale: Cover is managed through timing, route choice, and the Outskirts. Moving a message through official Courier Network channels has high Route but visible Cover — the Network is neutral but everyone knows what the Network carries. Moving it through the Outskirts has lower Route reliability but higher Cover — fewer people can see or intercept what moves through unofficial channels, at the cost of Outskirts visibility to the Broker.

Starting Momentum

At the start of a caravan encounter the GM sets starting Momentum by scoring each component on a 0 to 4 scale and summing them. The total maps directly to the 0–10 Momentum range — a perfect score of 12 maps to Momentum 10, reflecting that truly optimal conditions are rare and the scale accounts for that.

Heirs can invest before a caravan encounter begins — cultivating the route, vetting the courier, establishing better cover. These are political actions in the T1 and T2 range that translate directly into higher component scores and therefore higher starting Momentum when the caravan encounter activates.

Caravan Events

Every event in a caravan encounter is a choice about what to spend. Momentum, time, leverage, or safety. Usually some combination of all four.

Caravan encounters are structured as a sequence of events between origin and destination. Each event is a situation the caravan encounters in transit that requires a decision. Events affect Momentum — usually reducing it when handled poorly, sometimes increasing it when handled well, occasionally doing both. The movement arrives at its destination when Momentum reaches a successful resolution or fails when Momentum drops to zero before arrival.

Five event types cover the range of situations a caravan encounter produces.

Obstacle
Something Blocks the Path A physical, institutional, or social barrier between the caravan and its next progress point. At city scale this is a Watch checkpoint that wants to inspect the package, a district border where the controlling house has posted someone who asks questions, or a gathering in the Outskirts that makes a specific route temporarily impassable. Obstacles cost Momentum to push through directly or cost time to route around. Routing around costs Route component. Resolution paths: Direct transit if leverage with the controlling faction is sufficient. Alternative route if Route component can sustain the detour. Bribery or favor expenditure to clear the obstacle. Negation magic to suppress whatever is creating the blockage. Each path has a Momentum cost and some have political costs.
Threat
Something Is Pursuing An active hostile interest that is following or intercepting the caravan. At city scale this is another heir's agent following a courier, a Watch detail responding to a tip about what is being transported, or an Outskirts figure who has decided the package is worth taking. Threats activate chase mechanics — the caravan's Momentum races against the pursuit's pressure. Resolution paths: Maintain Momentum above the pursuit threshold until arrival. Create a diversion that eliminates the pursuit. Transposition magic to remove the caravan from the pursuit's path. Turn and confront the threat — which ends the caravan encounter and begins a combat or political encounter depending on who the threat is. Each path has different consequences for Cover.
Complication
Something Internal Goes Wrong A problem that originates within the caravan itself. At city scale this is a courier who has been approached by another heir and is considering the offer, a sealed document that arrives with evidence of having been opened, a contact who was supposed to facilitate transit but is no longer available. Complications attack Cohesion directly — they are the politics system reaching into the caravan system to create pressure. Resolution paths: Divination magic to determine whether the courier has been compromised before the message is handed over. Dominion magic to reinforce Cohesion — double-flagged, discovery risk. Replace the compromised element before it reaches the destination. Accept the Cohesion loss and push through with reduced Momentum. Complications that are not resolved become leverage for whoever created them.
Opportunity
Something Useful Appears A caravan encounter that offers something beyond reaching the destination. At city scale this is a courier who, while moving through the Scholarly District, overhears something valuable. A route that passes through a faction's territory and creates an opportunity to make contact. A package being delivered that creates a natural moment to establish relationship with the recipient beyond the formal delivery. Opportunities cost nothing but time to exploit — and time in a caravan encounter is Momentum. Resolution paths: Exploit the opportunity at the cost of Momentum — the caravan slows while the heir engages. Pass the opportunity and maintain Momentum. Divination school read to determine whether the opportunity is genuine or a setup. Opportunities that are exploited generate leverage movement outside the normal political action system — they are bonuses from the caravan encounter that feed back into the politics chapter.
Intelligence
Information the Caravan Carries or Encounters The caravan itself becomes the source of intelligence — either what it is carrying surfaces as more significant than initially known, or the caravan encounters information mid-transit that changes the situation. At city scale this is a sealed message that the recipient's response reveals was misdirection, a courier who arrives with additional information they gathered while moving, or a route that passes through territory where something is visibly wrong. Intelligence events do not affect Momentum directly — they affect what happens after the caravan arrives. Resolution paths: Act on the intelligence mid-transit, which costs Momentum and may change the destination or purpose of the movement. Deliver the caravan as planned and use the intelligence separately. Suppress the intelligence — Negation school. Share the intelligence with another heir, which creates a political moment with its own leverage implications. Intelligence events connect the caravan system to the politics system most directly.

Failure States

A caravan that fails to arrive is not always a total loss. What happens to the momentum when it collapses determines what can be recovered and what cannot.

When Momentum reaches zero before the caravan arrives the encounter fails. Five failure states describe what that failure produces — the specific consequence depends on how Momentum collapsed and which component drove the failure.

Delayed The caravan arrives but late. The timing was the point — a message that was supposed to arrive before a main session meeting arrives after it. The delivery still completes. Whatever the message was meant to accomplish before the meeting must now be accomplished differently, if at all.
Compromised The caravan arrives but something in it has been read, copied, or altered in transit. A message was opened and resealed. A package was inspected. The recipient receives what was sent but the sender does not know whether the contents are still confidential. Whoever compromised the caravan now has information they were not supposed to have.
Damaged Something in the caravan was physically lost or destroyed in transit. A document that cannot be replaced. Resources that cannot be recovered. The specific damage depends on what the caravan was carrying and how the Momentum collapse was produced. Damaged caravans require the heir to reconstruct whatever was lost — which costs political actions rather than caravan Momentum.
Captured The caravan and everything in it is now in someone else's possession. This is the failure state with the highest political consequence — whoever captured the caravan controls its contents, knows what was being moved, and can use that knowledge as leverage. A captured message in arc one's political environment is a political weapon.
Scattered The caravan fragmented — different elements went different directions under pressure and arrival cannot be confirmed for any of them. Some of what was sent may have arrived. Some may have been captured. Some may be delayed. The heir does not know which elements succeeded until information begins returning, which may take one or more sessions. Scattered is the most information-expensive failure state.

Chase and Escort

A chase is a caravan encounter with a clock. An escort is a caravan encounter with a floor. Both are the same system under different pressure.

Chase Sequences

When a caravan encounter activates a Threat event that moves into active pursuit, the encounter shifts to chase mechanics. Chase is a Momentum race — the caravan's current Momentum against a pursuit score set by the GM based on the nature and capability of what is pursuing. Each exchange the caravan either increases its lead or loses ground. If the pursuit score catches the caravan's Momentum before arrival the caravan is caught and the encounter transitions to whatever the pursuit was designed to produce — combat, capture, or confrontation.

Chase sequences at city scale are fast. A courier moving through familiar district routes to a destination one quarter of the city away does not have ten exchanges before they arrive. The GM sets the distance in exchanges based on the actual route and sets the pursuit's speed based on what is following. The heir managing the chase has fewer exchanges to work with than they expect. Every exchange counts.

Magic in chase sequences: Transposition at tier two or three can remove the caravan from a pursuit's path entirely — the courier steps to a location outside the pursuit's awareness and the chase ends because there is nothing to follow. This costs Momentum in willstrain but may be the cleanest resolution available. Divination can read the pursuit's most likely next move, allowing the caravan to route around before contact. Negation can suppress the trail — scent, sound, visible movement — making the caravan harder to track without eliminating the pursuit entirely.

Escort Missions

An escort mission is a caravan encounter where Momentum must remain above a floor threshold throughout the entire transit. The caravan cannot recover from a dip below the floor — a delegation that loses Cohesion at the midpoint has failed the escort regardless of whether it arrives intact. The floor represents what the mission requires to be considered successful by whoever commissioned it.

At city scale escort missions are diplomatic in nature — an heir who has agreed to ensure a contact reaches a specific meeting safely, a delegation from one house to another that must arrive with full Cohesion intact to have the authority to negotiate. The Momentum floor is set by the political stakes of the arrival, not by the physical difficulty of the route. A diplomatically critical delivery through friendly territory might have a higher floor than a physically dangerous route with low political stakes — because the standards for success are set by what the recipient needs to receive, not by what the sender needs to deliver.

Caravan at City Scale — Arc One

The Courier Network carries messages. The politics chapter explains their neutrality. The caravan chapter explains what it costs when that neutrality is insufficient for what you need to move.

In arc one the caravan system governs the movement of information through Shemsara. This section provides the specific framework for reading city-scale caravan encounters against the political landscape of the capital.

The Courier Network as Route Infrastructure

The Courier Network is the default Route for any information movement in Shemsara. Their neutrality means a message carried by the Network can transit any district regardless of which house controls it — the Network's neutrality extends to their physical movement through the city. Using the Network gives high Route component and visible Cover — everyone who cares knows the Network carries messages and knows approximately what that means about the heir's communications.

An heir who needs Cover above what the Network provides uses the Outskirts. Outskirts channels lower Route reliability — the path is less certain, the arrival less guaranteed — but what moves through the Outskirts does not appear in Network records. The tradeoff is visibility to the Broker, who tracks all Outskirts traffic without sharing it — unless sharing it becomes more valuable than holding it.

District Leverage and Route Component

Non-Network movement — a personal messenger, a house courier, a physical delivery that cannot go through official channels — has Route determined by district leverage. An heir moving something through their own district has full Route contribution from that leg of the journey. An heir moving something through a neutral district pays a small Route cost. An heir moving something through a rival heir's district without sufficient leverage with the controlling house faces a significantly compromised Route — the district creates friction, and friction costs Momentum.

Heirs who invest in cross-district relationships through political actions are investing in Route capacity for future caravan encounters. This is one of the ways the politics system and caravan system directly feed each other. High leverage across multiple districts makes the heir's information infrastructure more robust. Low leverage outside their own district makes them dependent on the Network for everything they need to move — and dependent on the Network's visible Cover for every movement.

Information as the Caravan's Cargo

At city scale the cargo is almost always information — messages, documents, intelligence gathered through Lore or Divination, evidence, misinformation planted through Outskirts channels. Information cargo creates specific considerations the caravan system is designed to handle.

Information can be copied without being taken — a Compromised failure state does not mean the message did not arrive, only that someone else now has it too. Information can be altered without being detected until the recipient acts on it. And information has a shelf life — a message that was valuable three days ago may be neutral now, which means a Delayed failure state is more consequential for information caravans than for resource caravans where the contents retain their value at a later arrival date.

Heirs who invest in Divination school capabilities gain the ability to verify information integrity at the point of receipt — determining whether a message was read in transit, whether the seal was genuine, whether the contents are what they appear to be. This is one of the most practically valuable applications of Divination in arc one's political environment.

Chapter Six
Exploration
Depth levels, chamber structure, and what the previous civilizations left in the dark.
The previous civilizations did not disappear. They became the ground the current one is built on. Everything the heirs do in Shemsara rests on foundations that nobody fully understands. The ruins are where those foundations are visible.

Three civilizations preceded Aetheria Nova. Each left physical traces — structures, mechanisms, records, and things that still operate without anyone maintaining them. Those traces are the ruins. The exploration encounter type is the mechanics for entering them.

Exploration is the encounter type where what you know matters more than what you can do. The heir who understood what they were walking into has a different encounter than the one who did not.

Exploration is structurally different from the other three encounter types. Politics is relational — it happens between people. Combat is immediate — it happens in exchanges. Caravan is directional — it happens across distance. Exploration is spatial and historical — it happens inside something that was built by people who understood different things than the heirs do, for reasons that may not be immediately apparent, in ways that create genuine danger for people who treat the space as merely physical.

Lore aspect choices in session zero are the primary preparation for exploration. They are not the only preparation. A caster with strong Divination school development has extraordinary advantages in ruins — the ability to read what a space was used for, trace resonance signatures left by the previous civilization's activity, and identify what something does before touching it. A Combat-trained heir has different advantages — they can survive what emerges from the deeper levels. What no heir can fully prepare for is depth they have not yet reached.

The Three Civilizations

The Scholars Guild has records on all three. The Archivist General decides what is accessible. The decision is consistent — information that would send heirs into ruins unprepared is withheld. Information that would send them in prepared is available, at a price.

Three previous civilizations left ruins in Aetheria Nova's territory. Each created different kinds of spaces, left different kinds of rewards, and created different kinds of dangers. An heir who knows which civilization built a specific ruin before entering it has significantly better odds of reading the space correctly.

Architects
The Architects Built Machines · Highest Spell Fragment Density The Architects built things that still operate. Mechanisms whose purpose is unclear, structures that maintain themselves without apparent upkeep, devices that respond to will in ways that suggest magical integration so deep it has become structural. An Architect ruin is not abandoned — it is still running. The question is what it is running and what triggers it to respond to the heirs' presence. Rewards: Highest density of spell fragments in any civilization's ruins. The Architects' integration of will into physical structure produces fragment concentrations that can, when assembled, yield spells unavailable through the Tier One Library. Technique scrolls that extend weapon specialties beyond what session zero training defined. Materials usable for advancement at higher tiers. Danger profile: The mechanisms. An Architect mechanism that activates in response to the heir's presence is not hostile by design — it is responsive. Understanding what response the mechanism was built to produce is the critical skill. A Lore heir with Magical Theory study has a significant advantage here. A Divination caster can read what the mechanism is designed to do before it does it.
Predecessor Courts
The Predecessor Courts Built Institutions · Legitimacy-Relevant Documents The Predecessor Courts were a political civilization — their ruins are administrative in character. Record rooms, council chambers, succession documents, and the physical infrastructure of a governing body that once controlled this territory. What they left is primarily information. Information that the current political order has strong reasons to manage carefully. Rewards: Documents that predate Aetheria Nova's founding and the political claims that founding was built on. Historical records that the Scholars Guild's public archive does not contain — because the Archivist General chose not to include them. Legitimacy-relevant materials that could strengthen or complicate specific houses' political claims. An heir who finds the right Predecessor Court document holds leverage that operates entirely outside the normal politics system. Danger profile: Other interested parties. Predecessor Court ruins are not physically dangerous in the way Architect ruins are — the danger is that someone else wants what the heir found, or does not want the heir to have found it. Every Predecessor Court discovery creates a caravan encounter to get the find out and a political encounter to determine who knows what was found.
Void Touched
The Void Touched Made Contact · Most Dangerous · D5-D6 Spaces The third civilization's name does not appear in the Scholars Guild's public records. What does appear is a consistent pattern in the historical record — a civilization that existed, that encountered something, and that changed fundamentally in the aftermath of that encounter in ways that the surviving records describe but do not explain. Their ruins exist at the deepest levels. What they left is not understood well enough to be catalogued. Rewards: Unknown in advance. What Void Touched spaces contain cannot be predicted from outside. What has been recovered by previous explorers and survived to reach the Guild's restricted records suggests artifacts of extraordinary capability and ordinary description — things that look like what they look like and do things that are not what they look like. The reward density is potentially very high. The survivability rate of explorers who have gone deep into Void Touched spaces is not. Danger profile: The space itself. Void Touched ruins do not contain mechanisms or documents. They contain consequences of whatever contact was made — residual effects of something that is not of the world in the conventional sense. The axiom's normal behavior may not apply consistently. Magic used in these spaces produces results that require significant GM adjudication because the rulebook the axiom operates from may not be the rulebook in effect here.

The Depth System

Depth is not a measure of how far underground something is. It is a measure of how much the space has resisted being understood. Some things at great physical depth are shallow. Some things near the surface are very deep indeed.

Depth is the primary resource of the exploration encounter type. It measures the relationship between a space's danger level and the heir's current capability. Depth levels run D1 through D6, mirroring the magic tier system deliberately — a D1 space makes demands on a character comparable to what a T1 casting demands of a caster. A D6 space makes demands that very few characters can currently meet.

Solo Depth Capability

An heir's solo depth capability is derived from their session zero training. Total aspect investment across five years divided by two, rounded down, plus one — this produces a solo depth ceiling between D1 and D3 for most heirs at session start. This ceiling is not a wall. An heir can attempt spaces deeper than their solo ceiling. The attempt simply proceeds without the preparation the space was designed to require, which means the space produces consequences the heir is not equipped to read or respond to correctly.

Party exploration extends effective depth. Two heirs with complementary preparation — one Lore-heavy, one Combat-trained — can reach spaces neither could manage alone. The Lore heir reads what the space is doing. The Combat heir manages what the space produces. Divination school capability extends this further — an heir who can perceive the space's actual behavior in real time before committing to it has an advantage that preparation alone cannot fully replicate.

Chamber Structure

Every chamber in every ruin has the same structure because the structure reflects something true about how exploration works — you perceive what is there, you decide what to do about it, and then you find out whether your decision was correct.

Exploration encounters are structured as a sequence of chambers. Each chamber has three phases — the Read, the Challenge, and the Resolution. This structure applies at every depth level, though what each phase contains changes dramatically as depth increases.

Phase One — The Read
The heir enters the chamber and perceives what is present before committing to action. The Read phase is the heir's opportunity to use Divination spells, Lore knowledge, and careful observation to understand what the chamber contains before they engage with it. The GM describes what is visible, audible, and detectable without commitment. The heir decides what else to look for and how.
Phase Two — The Challenge
The heir commits to an approach based on what they read. The challenge is what the chamber produces in response to that commitment — an Architect mechanism activating, a Predecessor Court lock requiring credentials, a Void Touched space responding to the heir's presence in ways that require immediate adjudication. The challenge is not always hostile. Sometimes it is a puzzle. Sometimes it is a choice. Sometimes it is a direct physical threat. The challenge reflects what the chamber was built to do, which is not always what the current inhabitants want it to do.
Phase Three — The Resolution
The heir's response to the challenge produces an outcome. Resolution includes both what the heir achieves — the reward — and what the achievement costs. A chamber resolved with full information and appropriate capability costs less than one resolved by brute force or luck. The difference matters for deeper chambers — an heir who enters D3 having spent significant resources on the first two chambers arrives at the third with less capacity than one who read each previous chamber accurately and managed costs carefully.

Discovery Bonuses

Chambers that are resolved with demonstrably superior preparation — the heir understood the civilization that built the space, identified what the challenge was before it fully activated, and deployed the correct capability in response — produce a Discovery Bonus. Discovery Bonuses are additional rewards beyond the chamber's base reward: a second spell fragment, additional context that makes the next chamber easier to read, or information that feeds directly into the politics or caravan systems.

Discovery Bonuses are the exploration system's mechanism for rewarding investment. An heir who spent three Lore years studying the Architects gets more from an Architect ruin than one who walked in without that preparation. The system does not require that preparation — any heir can enter any D1 or D2 space. But it rewards the investment made in session zero in the encounter type designed for it.

Depth-Matching Bonus

An heir whose current capability exactly matches the depth level of the space they are in — not over-prepared, not under-prepared, but precisely at the level the space requires — receives an additional Depth-Matching Bonus. This represents the specific advantage of operating in territory that challenges you at your current edge rather than either territory you have outgrown or territory that is overwhelming you. The edge is where the most complete development happens.

Reward Economy

What an heir finds in a ruin is not random. It is what the civilization that built the space left there, which is what that civilization valued, which tells the heir something about what kind of people they were entering the space of.

Exploration rewards fall into four categories. Each category feeds back into other encounter types or into character development in specific ways.

Loot Physical objects recovered from the ruin — Architect mechanisms that can be removed and repurposed, Predecessor Court items of historical or material value, materials that the Merchant Consortium would consider significant trade goods. Loot is tracked on the play sheet under Assets and generates leverage with factions interested in what was found. The Watch Commander is interested in Architect mechanisms. The Archivist General is interested in Predecessor Court materials. The Merchant Consortium is interested in anything of material value.
Spell Fragments Partial magical recordings left in the structure of Architect ruins — residual will-impressions that are not complete spells but contain the elements of one. Spell fragments are collected across multiple exploration encounters and assembled into complete spells not available through the Tier One Library. A fragment from one Architect ruin may combine with a fragment from another to produce something neither contained alone. Fragment assembly is collaborative — the Sanctum registers newly assembled spells and can identify what fragments from their own archive might complete a partial set.
Technique Scrolls Physical documents containing technique descriptions beyond what session zero training defined. Technique scrolls are rare and civilization-specific — Architects produced scrolls describing the integration of will into physical movement, extending weapon specialties into hybrid territory. Predecessor Courts produced administrative techniques, negotiation methods, and political approaches that add depth to the politics encounter type's available actions. Technique scrolls require an heir whose training is compatible with the scroll's content. A scroll describing a Sword technique extension is usable only by a Sword-trained heir.
Tier Advancement Materials Specific materials from Architect deep systems that accelerate the conditions for tier advancement in magic — not by granting advancement, but by creating the conditions where the advancement questions become more answerable. An heir who has been at the Phase of Pressure for some time and then encounters specific Architect materials may find that what was unclear becomes clear not because the material taught them something but because it produced the right kind of pressure at the right moment. The GM uses these materials as narrative tools for advancement rather than mechanical shortcuts.

Reward Scaling by Depth

Reward quality scales with depth. A D1 Architect chamber produces tier one spell fragments. A D3 Architect chamber produces fragments that combine into tier two or three spells. A D4 Predecessor Court vault produces documents with kingdom-level political implications rather than administrative records. The heir who goes deeper gets more. The heir who goes deeper without preparation gets more and loses more in the process — the reward scaling does not pause for insufficient capability. The chamber produces what it produces. Whether the heir can carry it out intact is a different question.

Solo Session Rewards

Solo sessions can reach D1 exploration rewards. A solo session that includes a D1 exploration encounter produces the base reward for that chamber — no Discovery Bonus, no Depth-Matching Bonus, but the base fragment or document or loot item. This represents an heir who made time between main sessions to follow up on a lead, to revisit a known accessible space, or to conduct the kind of low-depth scouting that provides information for the party to act on in the next main session.

Magic in Exploration

Every school has a use in exploration. Some are obvious. Some are not. The heir who only brings their primary school into a ruin is less prepared than the one who has thought about what each school does to the specific problems a ruin produces.

Magic interacts with exploration across all six schools. Unlike politics where certain school applications carry flags, and combat where slot costs are the primary concern, exploration magic is primarily about the Read phase — using magic to perceive what the space contains before committing to the Challenge phase.

Divination is the exploration school. Read at tier one can determine present conditions — is this mechanism armed, is this document what it appears to be, is this passage structurally sound. Warmth detects living presence in a space before entry. Trace at tier one follows the resonance signature left by the civilization's activity — reading what a space was used for based on the will-impressions it absorbed over time. At higher tiers Divination can access specific historical moments in the space, read the probability of what the next chamber contains, and identify spell fragment locations within a space before physically searching it.

Alteration at tier two or three can treat wounds accumulated in exploration without requiring medical facility access — field treatment in a ruin when returning to the surface is not immediately viable. It can also modify the heir's physical state to better match what a specific depth level requires — adjusting perception, modifying sensory capacity, adapting to whatever the Void Touched spaces are doing to normal physical law.

Negation can suppress active mechanisms — not permanently, but long enough for the heir to pass through or examine what is beyond without triggering the mechanism's full response. This is the exploration application of a school primarily known for counter-casting. At tier two Negation can suppress a Predecessor Court lock's authentication requirement for a brief duration. At tier three it can suppress an Architect mechanism's activation threshold entirely for one exchange.

Transposition provides the emergency exit. At tier one Step moves the heir a short distance within line of sight — enough to clear a mechanism's activation zone before it completes its response. At higher tiers Transposition can extract the heir from a chamber that has produced a challenge they cannot resolve, returning them to a previous point in the ruin at willstrain cost. The cost is real. The alternative may be worse.

Conjuration at tier two or three can bring specific materials to the heir within the ruin — equipment, light sources, tools that were not carried in. This is logistical magic in an environment where logistics matter because carrying everything potentially needed into a multi-chamber ruin is not practical and what a specific chamber requires cannot always be predicted from outside.

Dominion has limited exploration applications. Architect mechanisms do not have wills. Predecessor Court documents do not respond to suggestion. Void Touched spaces respond to presence in ways that Dominion's framework of will-on-will does not cleanly engage. In ruins with guardians — remnant entities left by the previous civilization — Dominion may apply. The GM adjudicates whether a specific entity's will is structured in ways Dominion school can engage.

The Legitimacy Threshold
The arc one political win condition. Four requirements. Any method.

The legitimacy threshold is not a moral standard. It is a recognition standard. The Small Council does not ask how an heir accumulated their leverage — only whether they have accumulated enough of it in the right places.

The Four Requirements

King Hector at +2 or above The king must recognize the heir as a capable and legitimate political actor. This is the hardest requirement to satisfy and the hardest to fake. Hector reads people accurately. Approaches that work on his councillors often do not work on him. Demonstrated capability in any encounter type that produces visible results in the kingdom's interest is the most reliable path.
Three of six factions at +2 or above The Merchant Consortium, City Watch, Dockworkers Union, Courier Network, Church of the Axiom, and Scholars Guild. Any three. An heir who controls their district's natural faction has a head start on one. Two more must be cultivated through political action, commerce, lore study, diplomacy, or command — each aspect choice from session zero creates a natural pathway to one faction's recognition.
Two of five other councillors at +1 or above The five other house heirs on the council. An heir needs at least two of them to recognize their legitimacy — not as allies necessarily, but as peers worth acknowledging. This requirement creates pressure toward inter-heir relationship building and away from purely isolationist strategies.
One high-weight endorsement at +3 or above A single relationship of genuine depth with one powerful figure. King Hector, the Church of the Axiom, the Scholars Guild, or any NPC with institutional weight. A +3 score represents recognition that goes beyond transactional — the NPC has invested something in the heir's success. This is the requirement that most rewards consistent relationship-building over one-time leverage grabs.
The threshold rewards any method. An heir who achieves +2 with King Hector through military service and an heir who achieves it through information control have met the same requirement. The council does not investigate how you got there.
The legitimacy threshold is the arc one win condition. Achieving it does not end the campaign — it opens the next chapter. What comes after an heir earns their seat is a different kind of problem than earning it.

Capstone Specialization

Five years given entirely to one thing produces something breadth cannot — not a bigger number, a different kind of recognition.

An heir who chooses the same aspect across all five years of study unlocks a Capstone Specialization at the Awakening, in addition to whatever that aspect already produces year over year. This is not a reward for picking favorites — it is what total commitment costs and what it buys. The heir arrives known for one thing before the campaign has given them a single chance to prove it, and the houses adjacent to that domain notice the absence of attention elsewhere.

Each Capstone grants a named trait with a real leverage effect — most are usable once per session, representing a standing advantage the heir can call on rather than something that happens automatically.
